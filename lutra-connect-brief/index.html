1Executive summary

Lutra Connect is a secure clinical communication platform connecting optometry practices with vetted ophthalmologists. The flagship service, Advice & Guidance, lets an optometrist obtain a same-day consultant opinion on a patient case through a structured, audit-logged thread. Around this sit two-way structured referrals, image sharing, post-operative assessment workflows and outcome reporting formatted for the National Ophthalmic Database and PHIN.

Connect is in commercial use today. Thirty optometry practices and five private surgeons are active, with 80 per cent PROM completion across post-operative cataract cohorts. The platform is integrated live with the Semble EPR, has a signed agreement with Ocuco for distribution through its Acuitas 3 practice-management system and is in active conversations with Topcon, Medisoft, Compucare (Streets Heaver) and Carebit.

This document proposes a phased launch over May to October 2026. The plan combines three confirmed clinical events (RCOphth Congress, Devon LOC AGM, College of Optometrists Optometry Tomorrow), a press release built around a specific data-protection angle, a sponsored podcast slot on The Cataract Specialist and targeted LinkedIn paid social. The budget assumption is modest. The objective is to anchor the consultant roster and bring the first commercial cohort of optom practices onto paid plans by end of Q3.

The sections that follow cover the strategic context (Section 2), the product (Section 3), pricing (Section 4), market sizing (Section 5), defensibility (Section 6), the integration roadmap (Section 7), the launch plan (Section 8) and the principal risks (Section 9).

2Background and the problem

2.1The UK eye-care pathway

Most patients first present with a visual symptom at a high-street optometry practice. The optometrist examines them, identifies anything outside routine refraction and decides whether to manage it locally or refer onward. Referrals route either into the NHS (typically a hospital eye service) or into private ophthalmology. Once referred, the optometrist generally receives no further information about the patient's care.

Optometrists are regulated by the General Optical Council. Ophthalmologists are regulated by the General Medical Council and represented professionally by the Royal College of Ophthalmologists. The two professions train and practise separately, but their work overlaps heavily in conditions such as suspected glaucoma, age-related macular degeneration, post-operative cataract follow-up and uveitis. There is no shared electronic record and no consistent channel for clinical communication between them.

2.2The use of informal channels

In the absence of formal infrastructure, communication between optometrists and ophthalmologists routinely happens over WhatsApp, SMS, email and the telephone. Clinicians use these channels to share OCT scans, fundus photographs and clinical history when seeking a second opinion or directing a referral.

This practice is incompatible with UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. Patient data shared over consumer messaging platforms places the responsibility on the individual clinician, not the platform provider, who is the data controller for the disclosure.[1] A breach of this kind is reportable to the Information Commissioner's Office. Indemnity insurers have begun flagging the practice in their renewal questionnaires.

The informal channels also produce no audit trail, no consistent way to convert an advice request into a referral and no mechanism to feed outcome data back to the originating optometrist.

2.3What patients face today

A patient whose optometrist finds something concerning has, in practice, two options. They can join the NHS waiting list for a hospital appointment, typically several weeks or months. Or they can pay for a private consultation, which in the UK ophthalmology market generally costs between £200 and £400. There is no intermediate route.

This binary is the commercial opportunity behind Connect's Rapid Consultant Review feature, addressed in Section 3.

2.4Why now

Three factors make a launch in 2026 timely:

  1. NHS capacity strain. Hospital eye services are among the most over-subscribed outpatient specialties. Demand for any solution that triages, defers, or substitutes a hospital appointment is structurally rising.
  2. Regulatory awareness. The ICO has begun publicly warning clinicians about messaging-app use and indemnity insurers are following. The clinician audience is more receptive to a secure alternative than it was three years ago.
  3. Distribution partners in place. The Semble integration is live and the Ocuco partnership is signed. Both give access to the practice and consultant population without per-site procurement.

3The product

Positioning

"A consultant in every optometry practice." Each consultant is a real practising ophthalmologist, vetted by Lutra and indemnified for the advice given through the platform.

Connect is a single platform with one paid module (Advice & Guidance) and four supporting modules. Customers buy the platform, not the individual modules.

3.1Advice & Guidance, the flagship module

An optometrist initiates a structured thread, attaches the relevant images (OCT, fundus, fields) and clinical history and routes the case to a consultant by sub-specialty. The consultant responds, generally within hours, with a structured opinion. The thread is logged with full audit metadata and can be converted into a formal referral with one click. The current median consultant response time across active threads is in the order of hours, against NHS Advice & Guidance turnaround times typically measured in weeks.

The same workflow supports both NHS-funded Advice & Guidance (where commissioned) and patient-funded Rapid Consultant Review, in which the patient pays a fee for the specialist input, with the practice retaining a defined margin and the consultant receiving the balance. Working pricing for the patient-paid route is £40 to £70 per case, to be validated through the launch cohort.

3.2Wider shared-care modules

The remaining modules are bundled with the paid plan and drive retention.

  • Structured referrals. Two-way, formatted for the receiving service, with outcome data returned to the originator.
  • Image sharing. OCT, fundus and visual field attachments live on the clinical thread, with secure storage and in-context viewing.
  • Post-operative assessment. Shared follow-up workflows for cataract and other elective procedures, with complication capture.
  • Outcomes reporting. Patient-reported outcomes (PROMs) and experience measures (PREMs), captured directly from the patient and pre-formatted for export to the National Ophthalmic Database and PHIN.
Current usage

Across the active cohort, post-operative PROM completion stands at approximately 80 per cent. Industry benchmarks for paper or email-led PROM collection are typically in the 30 to 40 per cent range. The figure is a meaningful proof point for the wider stack rather than the flagship A&G module.

4.1Customer segments

Connect has three paying customer segments. Pricing is structured to keep the optometry side flywheel-fast, the consultant side high-value and the private hospital side on annual contract terms.

4.2Revenue logic

Revenue arrives from three sources. Subscription forms the base. Transaction take on patient-paid cases compounds on top of subscription as practice usage matures. Enterprise contracts deliver the largest single contributions and the highest margin, but with longer sales cycles.

The pricing assumption is that the per-practice rather than per-optometrist model removes a procurement objection and accelerates initial sign-up. The trade-off is lower headline ARPA at small sites; we expect this to be offset by transactional revenue from patient-paid cases. The model will be reviewed against actual usage data after the first six months.

6Defensibility

Lutra's defensibility rests on the network of vetted clinicians rather than the underlying software. The argument has three components.

6.1The marketplace dynamic

Connect's defensibility has two layers. The first is Lutra's own platform: purpose-built for the shared-care workflow between optometry and ophthalmology and currently ahead of competing systems on functionality, integrations and clinical UX. The second is the marketplace of clinicians on the platform. This is the harder layer for a competitor to reproduce.

A marketplace of vetted, indemnified consultants on one side and active optometry practices on the other is harder to replicate than software in the age of vibe coding. On the consultant side, that means a roster of practising ophthalmologists vetted by Lutra, indemnified for the advice given through the platform and willing to respond to threads within hours. On the optometry side, it means practices using Connect in daily clinical work, with the workflow embedded into their PMS through Ocuco.

Both sides are built through individual clinical introductions, time at conferences and personal vetting of each clinician. A fast-follower with similar code and more capital still has to do that work one clinician at a time.

6.2Integrations as distribution

Integrations into the PMS, EPR and diagnostic platforms reduce switching cost for clinicians and expand the addressable practice base without per-practice procurement. The Ocuco agreement, in particular, gives access to enterprise optometry through a system the practices already use.

6.3Multi-jurisdictional scope

The architecture supports onboarding and routing clinicians across borders, provided we operate within each market's regulatory framework. The initial geographic priority is the UK, with Australia as the planned second activation in 2026 to 2027. Other English-first markets follow. This is not a UK-only product and the addressable market in Section 5 reflects that.

The lead message is the data-protection angle, because it is both the most differentiating and the one trade press is most likely to publish. 8.2Channels and tactics

Press and earned media

The launch press release is built around clinician data-protection risk rather than a generic product launch announcement. We will commission a short piece of original research (a survey of UK optometrist messaging habits) to give one trade outlet an exclusive headline they want to publish. Distribution targets are Optometry Today, Optician, Eye News and the Health Service Journal. A founder op-ed authored by Sam Evans or Conor Ramsden will run in parallel.

Paid social

LinkedIn is the primary paid channel. Spend is modest. Two separate ad sets, with distinct creative and landing pages, run against optometrists and ophthalmologists respectively. Optometrist creative leads on the data-protection angle and the patient-paid revenue stream. Ophthalmologist creative leads on the structured private patient channel and NOD-formatted outcomes export. Event-targeted bursts run in the two weeks before RCOphth Congress and Optometry Tomorrow; always-on retargeting continues throughout.

Events

Three confirmed events anchor the calendar: RCOphth Congress in May, the Devon LOC AGM in May and the College of Optometrists Optometry Tomorrow in June. For each, the operating model is the same: pre-event LinkedIn targeting of registered attendees, a two-minute founder-led demo loop on the stand, QR-coded literature pointing to event-specific landing pages for attribution and structured email follow-up within 48 hours of the event. Remaining speaker and sponsored-symposium slots at RCOphth Congress should be checked; speaker slots tend to convert at multiples of stand traffic.

Podcast

Lutra is the ongoing sponsor of Simon Madge's The Cataract Specialist, with no fixed end date on the arrangement. Beyond the sponsorship read itself, the higher-value opportunity is offering Sam Evans or Conor Ramsden as a guest. An interview timed to drop before RCOphth Congress, clipped into 60-second LinkedIn assets afterwards, makes better use of the relationship than the standing sponsorship slot alone.

Owned and earned

The Local Optical Committee (LOC) network is the most under-used distribution asset in UK optometry. There are roughly 70 LOCs in England, most with newsletters and regular AGMs. Target six to eight LOC speaking slots by year-end. A monthly CPD-accredited webinar series, titled along the lines of Patient data, WhatsApp and what the GOC expects, drives steady optometrist sign-up at near-zero cost and creates recurring inbox presence. A formal advocate programme of ten to fifteen named clinicians, equipped with shareable monthly assets, distributes endorsement through their own networks.

9Key risks

The principal risks to the plan, with the mitigations currently in train.

Risk Description Mitigation
Two-sided cold start An insufficient consultant roster makes the optom-side value proposition hollow; an insufficient optom user base makes the consultant proposition hollow. The 12-month free Consultant Plan for the first 50 accounts is designed to anchor the consultant side ahead of optom rollout. Direct named outreach to 30–50 anchor consultants is being executed by founders.
Regulatory and indemnity Indemnification of consultants for advice given through the platform requires careful structuring. A failure in indemnity cover would expose individual consultants. Indemnity arrangements reviewed by external counsel. Audit log architecture is DCB0129/DCB0160 aligned. Clinical Safety Officer is nominated.
Competitive landscape Cinapsis, EyeV and similar UK platforms operate in adjacent territory but are purely referral systems, not A&G. They do not provide the same-day consultant opinion workflow that Connect is built around. A well-funded entrant could attempt to follow Connect's two-sided A&G model. The marketplace dynamic and integration partnerships (Section 6) provide lead time. Speed of consultant roster build is the key variable.
Consumer AI substitution Patients are increasingly using generative AI tools like ChatGPT for medical advice. This is free and immediate, which puts it in direct competition with Rapid Consultant Review. The dynamic mirrors clinicians' use of WhatsApp: in both cases convenience and zero cost outweigh awareness of the underlying risks. Many patients are not familiar with the clinical limitations of consumer AI for health questions; AI quality will also continue to improve. Rapid Consultant Review is built around access to a named human consultant indemnified for the advice given. Many patients still value the opinion of a real clinician, particularly on findings raised by their optometrist from diagnostic imaging. Pricing at £40 to £70 keeps a real consultant accessible alongside whatever AI advice the patient might also be using.
Integration partner dependency The Ocuco and Semble integrations support distribution but Connect is not solely reliant on either. Disruption to either relationship could reduce one of the practice or consultant onboarding channels, with knock-on impact on uptake and retention in that segment. Active conversations with Topcon, Medisoft, Compucare and Carebit diversify the partner base. No single partner is structurally required for the launch.
Pricing validation The £40–£70 patient-paid case price and the £49 per-practice Practice Plan are working assumptions. Actual willingness to pay is not yet validated at scale. The launch cohort is the validation mechanism. Pricing review is built into the September–October cohort review (Section 8.3).
Founder bandwidth The launch combines product, commercial, event and content workload across a small founding team. Execution quality risks dropping if everything runs in parallel. Sequencing of the plan (lead, then sustain) is partly a workload-management decision, not only a marketing one. Hiring priorities for Q3 reflect this.
