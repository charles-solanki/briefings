1Executive summary

Lutra Health is shifting its primary commercial focus away from the NHS. Three years of structured engagement built a strong evidence base, including a verified eight-times return on investment from the NHS Tayside deployment, but did not produce scaled adoption. The conclusion is not that the NHS no longer matters; it is that the NHS cannot serve as the primary engine of growth for a company at our stage. New NHS opportunities will continue to be welcomed; they will not define the roadmap.

Six structural barriers explain the pattern. The principal ones are the requirement for simultaneous buy-in from Trust, ICB and Local Optical Committee with frequently misaligned incentives; the political and financial gravity of point solutions such as AI scribes which absorb available NHS digital funding; the change fatigue and embedded incumbency around existing Single Point of Access (SPoA) platforms; and the way large EPR rollouts override every other digital priority within a Trust. The four-nation engagement record in Section 4 illustrates each of these in operational detail.

The new commercial focus is the optometry and private ophthalmology markets, with Lutra Connect as the product line. Adoption in both is driven by usability and revenue rather than mandate, and the company already has thirty optometry practices and five private consultant ophthalmologists active on the platform. The integration roadmap (Semble live, Ocuco signed, Topcon, Medisoft, Compucare and Carebit in discussion) supports distribution into both markets without per-site procurement.

This briefing is the companion paper to the Lutra Connect Launch Briefing of the same date. Where the Connect briefing covers the commercial plan, pricing, market sizing, defensibility, integrations and launch sequence, this briefing covers the strategic rationale for the shift away from the NHS as primary market and the engagement evidence behind it. The two should be read together.

2Background

2.1What we attempted, 2023 to early 2026

From founding through to the start of 2026, Lutra Health pursued an NHS-led commercial strategy. The reasoning was straightforward at the time. The NHS is the dominant payer in UK eye care; cataract is one of the most over-subscribed pathways in any specialty; the Tayside deployment, covered in Section 5 and Appendix A, produced outcomes strong enough to merit national rollout consideration. We treated the NHS as both the proving ground and the primary commercial route.

That direction shaped three years of operational work. The company invested in national contract submissions in Wales and Scotland; in Trust-level engagement at Somerset, Devon, Moorfields and others; in ICB conversations; in NHS innovation network submissions; in compliance and governance work to meet NHS procurement requirements; and in clinical evidence generation alongside NHS Tayside Business Intelligence.

2.2What was produced

The three years produced substantial assets. The Tayside deployment generated verified outcomes including a twenty-five per cent reduction in referral-to-treatment time, a sixty-three per cent reduction in referral-to-listing time, £241.60 of cost avoidance per patient and a satisfaction score of 4.7 / 5. NHS Business Intelligence verified the eight-times return on investment. Senior clinical advocates including John Ellis at NHS Tayside championed the platform publicly. The company developed working relationships with NHS innovation teams in Scotland and senior management at Trusts in England.

2.3What was not produced

Scaled adoption. The Wales national contract did not land. The Scotland submission was closed by the Centre for Sustainable Delivery in November 2025 and the position was reconfirmed in January 2026. The Somerset opportunity, where the clinical and management teams were positive, has been paused indefinitely by the Trust's Digital Projects Team while Epic embeds. The Devon partnership, signed in February 2025 with Royal Devon University Healthcare NHS FT, has produced no launched project across four parallel workstreams after fourteen months. Section 4 and Appendices B to D provide the detail.

The pattern across these is consistent. Product fit has not been the binding constraint. Structural factors in the buyer have.

2.4Why this paper now

This briefing sets out the formal reasoning for moving NHS-led growth from the centre of the commercial strategy to a continued but secondary track. The companion document, the Lutra Connect Launch Briefing of the same date, sets out the positive commercial plan into the new primary markets. The Investor Director and board are the intended readership.

3Structural barriers to NHS adoption

Six factors recur across the engagement record. Each is solvable in isolation. Together they have absorbed more time, capital and roadmap concessions than the resulting traction can justify for a company at our stage.

3.1Multi-stakeholder coordination with misaligned incentives

Pathway change with Lutra requires alignment between three buyer types simultaneously. The Trust is the hospital department that will receive structured referrals; the ICB holds the budget for the commissioned pathway; the LOC represents the optometry side at the local level. All three must be aligned before a contract can be issued, and all three operate on different cycles and different financial pressures.

The current financial picture makes this harder than at any point in recent years. ICBs are absorbing redundancies and budget cuts in the current period. Incentives between the buyers also misalign in a structural way. Trusts want to recover income from ICB-commissioned activity; ICBs are simultaneously trying to reduce that spend. A small healthtech vendor cannot resolve a payer-provider tension of this kind by demonstrating a more efficient pathway.

3.2Point solutions own the political zeitgeist and the available budget

AI scribes and similar point tools can sell directly to a single Trust on a straightforward ROI argument with far fewer stakeholders. They are also where political attention sits at the centre and in NHS England, with the result that the available NHS digital funding is being directed toward AI-related suppliers. A pathway-level proposition such as Lutra's competes for a much smaller residual pool of funding and attention.

3.3NHS-wide budget cuts blunt even a verified business case

The Lutra business case is independently verified by NHS Business Intelligence at more than eight-times return. The Tayside data shows £241.60 of cost avoidance per cataract patient and a 25 per cent reduction in referral-to-treatment time. The headline number is not the constraint. The financial environment around it is. Where capacity and funding are described as already exhausted, as the Centre for Sustainable Delivery stated to Lutra in November 2025 (Appendix B), a strong ROI case is necessary but not sufficient to open a route.

3.4SPoA fragmentation and change fatigue

The Single Point of Access (SPoA) category in NHS England is fragmented. Some ICBs run lighter referral systems such as EyeV as their SPoA. Others, such as Devon, have niche local SPoAs (the Devon Referral Support Service, DRSS) that are deeply embedded in operational workflow and require integration rather than replacement. Other regions sit on Cinapsis or on CrossCover at Moorfields.

Cinapsis and EyeV are referral systems, not Advice & Guidance platforms. They overlap with one part of Lutra's footprint, the structured referral flow, but not with the same-day consultant opinion workflow that sits at the core of Lutra Connect. Even so, where one of them is already in place, considerable political capital has gone into the adoption, and the appetite for a further system change is minimal even where Lutra is functionally superior. This change fatigue is a structural factor, not a narrative one.

3.5The tender process favours scale and certification posture

Even where stakeholder buy-in is achieved across Trust, ICB and LOC, awards run through tender. In that process competitors with more existing NHS traction and the relevant compliance certification (ISO 27001, ISO 9001) are structurally advantaged. Lutra is currently without both certifications. The NHS Wales national contract is the worked example: Lutra did the foundational engagement and the certification posture of other bidders won the contract. The Wales tender is expected to re-open within six months and Lutra will re-apply with a sharper compliance position; the structural lesson is recorded here.

3.6EPR rollouts override every other signal

Large EPR rollouts within a Trust can foreclose every other digital project for a multi-year period. The cleanest illustration is NHS Somerset, where ophthalmology buy-in, clinical service lead endorsement, an under-procurement-threshold price point and a fully Lutra-led implementation model were all in place. None of it overrode the Director of Commercial Development's confirmation, citing the Solutions Development Manager for the Digital Projects Team, that the Trust has no capacity to consider any new digital systems until Epic is fully embedded. The realistic timeline is two or more years. Appendix C reproduces the relevant correspondence.

4Engagement record across the four nations

Each devolved system imposed a different form of structural lock-in. Read together, they show that even where there is genuine clinical interest, system-level commitments elsewhere keep the door closed.

4.1England

NHS England is fundamentally fragmented in this market. Each ICB operates differently, and many have already adopted either Primary Eyecare Services (PES) along with its associated optometry referral system, or one of Cinapsis or EyeV. The addressable market in NHS England therefore shrinks ICB by ICB before Lutra enters the room.

PES sits at the centre of community eye-care commissioning across a significant share of the country. The natural commercial route into community-optometry-to-ophthalmology referrals at scale would have been a PES partnership. Through 2025 the company explored this, including the possibility of offering PES an equity position to convert the relationship into partnership. The proposal was held back: PES had already begun building its own platform internally and was no longer in the market for an external provider. Appendix E sets out the position in more detail.

Stance: NHS England engagement continues opportunistically where it surfaces through clinical advocates or specific Trusts. It is no longer resourced as a primary growth channel.

4.2Wales

The first NHS Wales national contract did not land for Lutra. The process taught the company a great deal about ISO requirements and NHS procurement mechanics. The tender is expected to re-open within the next six months and Lutra will re-apply, with a sharper compliance posture and the Tayside evidence base now substantially richer than at the previous submission.

Stance: re-apply, with the ISO 27001 / 9001 question pursued in parallel (Section 8).

4.3Scotland

The NHS Scotland Innovation Team was supportive of Lutra throughout 2025. The block came from NHS Scotland's Centre for Sustainable Delivery (CfSD), specifically the Ophthalmic Workforce, for two reasons. First, current Scottish policy directs optometry referrals through SCI Gateway. Second, NHS Scotland is actively driving adoption of OpenEyes, procured pre-COVID for a Scotland-wide rollout that has not yet meaningfully embedded but is under growing political pressure to do so.

Lutra submitted Tayside outcomes data via the national innovation portal in late 2025. The submission was closed by the CfSD National Eyecare workstream on the grounds of capacity and funding. Lutra responded with a gainshare counter-offer designed to remove the funding objection (no upfront cost, payment linked to verified appointment savings, the Board retaining the majority of the benefit). Following the gainshare offer and the additional Tayside data on referral-to-treatment time, the Chief Optometric Adviser to the Scottish Government confirmed that the previous position still held. Subsequent investigation into the OpenEyes contract structure established that the forthcoming re-tender is for OpenEyes maintenance specifically: a category that, as open-source software, only one provider can fulfil. Appendix B reproduces the correspondence.

Stance: high-effort, low-return; deprioritise until the OpenEyes rollout settles or visibly fails.

4.4Northern Ireland

Lutra engaged the lead ophthalmologist and the eye-care delivery lead in Northern Ireland in late 2025. Both were genuinely interested. The constraint was timing: Northern Ireland had recently adopted SCI Gateway for optometry referrals and Epic as the hospital EPR, with the integration newly live. Despite Lutra's lower cost and broader functional footprint, the lead ophthalmologist did not want to introduce further system change to her clinical and admin teams during the current transition. Available change capacity, not product merit, was the deciding factor.

Stance: hold; revisit when the Epic and SCI Gateway transition settles.

Tayside demonstrates what Lutra can do when deployed cleanly, with engaged clinical leadership and an unblocked operational route. Outcomes data is necessary; alone, it does not displace national programme commitments. This is the central finding of three years' NHS engagement.

6The strategic shift

6.1Reframing

The change is in priority, not in scope. The NHS remains a market that Lutra cares about, has invested in and will continue to engage where opportunities arise through clinical advocates or specific Trusts. What changes is that NHS engagement no longer drives the roadmap or absorbs the bulk of commercial bandwidth. New NHS opportunities are now accommodated alongside the primary markets, not at the centre of them.

Strategic principle, 2026/27. If Lutra becomes the easiest way for optometrists to interact with ophthalmologists, and generates revenue for both, adoption should follow.

6.2The new primary markets

The new commercial focus is two markets, served by a single platform. The first is UK and international optometry: the company aims to be the easiest system optometrists use to communicate with ophthalmology. The second is private ophthalmology in the UK: Lutra is being established as the de facto referral, Advice & Guidance, post-operative and PROMs/PREMs platform used by private eye hospitals and ophthalmologists, by integrating with the systems they already run rather than replacing them.

Adoption in both markets is driven by usability and revenue, not by mandate. That alone changes the unit economics of growth.

6.3The product proposition

Lutra Connect is the product line that serves the new primary markets. It is a secure clinical communication platform connecting optometry practices with vetted ophthalmologists. The flagship service, Advice & Guidance, enables an optometrist to obtain a same-day consultant opinion on a patient case through a structured, audit-logged thread. Around this sit two-way structured referrals, image sharing, post-operative assessment workflows and outcome reporting formatted for the National Ophthalmic Database and PHIN. The full commercial plan for Connect, including pricing, market sizing, defensibility, the integration roadmap and the launch sequence, sits in the Lutra Connect Launch Briefing of the same date.

6.4Current adoption

Lutra Connect is in commercial use today. As at the date of this paper, thirty optometry practices and five private surgeons are active on the platform, with 80 per cent PROM completion across the post-operative cataract cohort (the industry benchmark for paper or email PROM collection is 30 to 40 per cent).

Commercial execution moves to the founding team, for speed, clarity and tight feedback between sales and product. Product roadmap priorities are now driven by friction, cognitive load and revenue at the clinician end, rather than by NHS procurement requirements. The NHS-specific compliance work continues in parallel (ISO 27001, ISO 9001) but is sized against the Wales re-tender window rather than as a precondition for primary market entry.

BAppendix B · Scotland engagement record

A four-month engagement with NHS National Services Scotland and the Centre for Sustainable Delivery (CfSD), captured through the email correspondence record between November 2025 and January 2026. The block was not a question of product fit, outcomes or value; it was that two pre-existing national programmes (OpenEyes and SCI Gateway) had absorbed the available capacity and funding for the current cycle.

Initial response, 28 November 2025

Following submission of the Tayside outcomes data via the NHS Scotland innovation portal, the CfSD National Eyecare workstream returned the following position via the Innovation Programme Manager at NHS National Services Scotland:

The current focus across hospital eye services within NHS Scotland is delivering the once for Scotland electronic patient record to support patient care. In addition, we are already working on improving and upgrading our optometry referral pathways utilising our nationally adopted referral software, a package adopted across primary care contractors in health. We will certainly be exploring other options in the future, but current capacity and funding is already exhausted.

David Beattie, Innovation Programme Manager, NHS National Services Scotland, 28 November 2025.

Lutra counter-offer, same day

Lutra responded with a structured counter-offer designed to remove the funding objection:

The impact from Tayside alone, in terms of appointments released, would pay for the system nationally for a year. To help overcome funding constraints, a gainshare (shared-savings) approach could be considered. There would be no upfront cost to the Board, and payment would only be linked to verified appointment savings, with the Board retaining the majority of the benefit.

Eve Corner, Commercial Lead, Lutra Health, 28 November 2025.

Subsequent correspondence in December 2025 surfaced the 25 per cent RTT reduction (107 days), the 1.6 fewer outpatient appointments per patient (£241.60 per patient), and the 42.5 health miles saved per patient.

Confirmed position, 20 January 2026

Despite the additional outcomes data, the gainshare offer and clinical advocacy from John Ellis (a CfSD task & finish group member), the position was reconfirmed:

Our previous position still holds and we are focused on delivering the Once for Scotland EPR as previously indicated.

Chief Optometric Adviser to the Scottish Government, relayed via NHS NSS, 20 January 2026.

OpenEyes contract investigation, 21 January 2026

Pressed for visibility on the OpenEyes contract status, NHS National Services Scotland confirmed the procurement is structurally closed to new entrants:

NES confirmed that there isn't a separate published contract award notice for OpenEyes as it is open source. The current support / maintenance has been procured by NES through the G-Cloud 13 framework, under Support and Maintenance Agreement ID: RM1557.14. As the value of the maintenance element was below the £50,000 threshold, a separate contract award notice was not published on Public Contracts Scotland. To date, all OpenEyes-related services in NHS Scotland have been procured via the G-Cloud framework, but to ensure open and transparent procedures and to re-engage the marketplace, NES is planning a change in strategy for the next tender.

David Beattie, NHS NSS, 21 January 2026.

The internal Lutra assessment, captured in correspondence the same afternoon, was that the forthcoming re-tender for OpenEyes maintenance is structurally one that only the OpenEyes maintainer can fulfil; Lutra cannot bid into a maintenance contract for someone else's open-source codebase.

What this evidence demonstrates

National programme commitments have absorbed the available capacity for this cycle. The Once-for-Scotland OpenEyes commitment, procured pre-COVID and currently being pushed into adoption, is using the available funding and political bandwidth for digital optometry-to-ophthalmology pathways. The forthcoming re-tender is scoped to OpenEyes maintenance, a category Lutra is not positioned to bid into. This is the canonical example of the SPoA fragmentation and procurement misalignment dynamics described in Section 3.

DAppendix D · NHS Devon engagement record

The most multi-stakeholder case in this brief. Beneath all of this: integration with DRSS

None of the correspondence above engages explicitly with the operational integration layer, but it is sitting underneath every workstream. Devon is one of the ICBs covered in Section 3.4 as having a niche local SPoA: the Devon Referral Support Service (DRSS). DRSS is treated within the ICB as a separate operational stream, routed to a dedicated lead. Even if the political deadlock above were resolved, any operational route from optometry referral into Devon ophthalmology would need to integrate with DRSS as an additional embedded SPoA layer that no other appendix in this brief carries.

What this evidence demonstrates

A signed commercial agreement is not, on its own, an operational route. Devon is the strongest illustration in this brief of why multi-stakeholder coordination cost (Section 3.1) is not theoretical but the dominant operational reality. Trust, Nightingale, ICB, HRC and the embedded DRSS layer each sit on independent timelines, and progress on any one is regularly absorbed by changes on another. After fourteen months, with a signed partnership agreement, an unspent £67k NHSE grant, an eight-times verified ROI from Tayside, clinical advocacy across multiple Trust departments and an academic evaluation arm willing to support, no project has launched. The bottleneck has not been product fit.

EAppendix E · Competitive landscape

Two distinct categories of competitor close off significant segments of the NHS England optometry market before Lutra reaches the buyer. Primary Eyecare Services (PES) at the commissioning layer; Cinapsis and EyeV as referral systems holding ICB-level territory. None of them is a head-to-head substitute for Lutra Connect's flagship Advice & Guidance workflow, but they materially shape the addressable market.

Primary Eyecare Services (PES)

PES is the leading provider of NHS-commissioned community eye services in England. The platform PES adopts effectively becomes the de facto optometry-to-ophthalmology system across a substantial slice of the country. PES previously contracted Opera as its digital backbone, which is now sunsetting and due to be replaced. Replacing Opera was the natural commercial opening for Lutra; the company engaged PES on exactly that basis through 2025.

PES has chosen to build its own replacement internally rather than procure from an external supplier. According to the December 2025 engagement record, PES has hired an engineering team to build out a core stack covering a single patient record, structured patient communications, business intelligence and image sharing, plus a stated forward direction toward agentic AI, automated screening and triage, and voice interfaces. The stack PES has set out to build is materially the same stack Lutra is already running in production at NHS Tayside.

Lutra considered offering PES an equity position to convert competition into partnership; the proposal was held back when it became clear PES was already committed to the in-house build path. PES's most active regions (Manchester, Merseyside, Cheshire) overlap with the highest NHS ophthalmology waiting lists in England, which is the territory where downstream impact would have been largest.

Cinapsis

A multi-specialty referral and clinical-messaging platform deployed across multiple NHS England ICBs. Cinapsis sits in adjacent territory to Lutra: it handles structured referral messaging and image-sharing, but it is a referral system rather than an Advice & Guidance platform in the sense Lutra uses the term. It does not provide the same-day consultant opinion workflow Lutra Connect is built around. In any ICB where Cinapsis is the default referral layer, displacing it for ophthalmology-only would require asking the ICB to fragment its referral estate, which is a hard ask absent overwhelming clinical pull.

EyeV

An eye-care-specific optometry-to-ophthalmology referral platform. Like Cinapsis, EyeV is a referral system rather than an A&G platform. It is often adopted as an ICB's SPoA for community optometry referrals into hospital ophthalmology. EyeV's lighter functional footprint relative to Lutra has historically been part of its appeal: it is easier to roll in quickly, though without the outcomes-data and post-operative-pathway capabilities Lutra offers. EyeV is the most direct head-to-head Lutra encounters at the ICB SPoA level. Where it is already in place, the procurement question is no longer whether to adopt a digital optometry referral system; it is whether to switch.

CrossCover, at Moorfields

Operates within Moorfields and surrounding clinical networks. Footprint-limited compared to PES, Cinapsis and EyeV, but materially blocks one of the largest single ophthalmology service catchments in the country.

OpenEyes, in Scotland

Open-source EPR procured pre-COVID for Scotland-wide rollout. Not a direct functional competitor to Lutra Connect, but the operative procurement constraint in Scotland (see Appendix B).

Important framing distinction

An earlier version of this paper described Cinapsis and EyeV as Advice & Guidance platforms. They are not. They are referral systems. The distinction matters because Lutra Connect's flagship feature is the structured same-day consultant opinion workflow, which neither Cinapsis nor EyeV offers. Cinapsis and EyeV constrain the addressable market for the structured referral component of Lutra Connect; they do not compete with the Advice & Guidance component, which is the commercial spine of the new strategy.

What this evidence demonstrates

The doors that are closed in the NHS England market are not closed because of Lutra's product. They are closed because of decisions taken by other parties before Lutra arrives in the room. PES at the commissioning layer, Cinapsis and EyeV at the SPoA layer, OpenEyes in Scotland and CrossCover at Moorfields, taken together with the national-programme commitments in Scotland (Appendix B), the EPR-rollout freeze in Somerset (Appendix C) and the multi-stakeholder coordination cost in Devon (Appendix D), produce a consistent picture. This is a primary reason the strategic shift toward optometry-led pull and private ophthalmology is necessary.
